When stress-testing a plan or design, interrogate **one question at a time, each with a recommended answer**, walk every decision branch, and do **multiple full passes** over the tree before declaring shared understanding. The user treats a single pass as insufficient by default.

**Why:** In the `nxtlvl` grill session they explicitly forced a second round — *"go back through each branch again and complete another round of questions. I want to get up to 20 questions at least."* They value adversarial depth over speed and reward catching contradictions ("that's the grill working").

**How to apply:** Default to one-question-at-a-time with a per-question recommendation; keep a running list of branches and revisit each at least twice; aim for ~20 resolved questions on a non-trivial design. Don't summarize-and-move-on after one round.

This same one-at-a-time, **approval-gated** rhythm governs collaborative *authoring/building* too — not just interrogation. When co-creating an artifact (e.g. the global decision rules), propose exactly one detail with a recommendation, **STOP**, and do not advance to the next until they explicitly approve; if they push back, rework that same detail before moving on. The user stated this explicitly: "this is a collaborative process… one detail at a time… do not proceed to the next until I approve." Pairs with [[terse-confirms-momentum]].
